My research group seeks to understand how cells build ribosomes, the machines that synthesize proteins from amino acids. We are primarily interested in the roles ribosomes play in development and disease. Ribosomes, intricate molecular machines, are best known for translating genomic information into proteins, building blocks of our cell-types and tissues. Human cells assemble millions of ribosomes to sustain their growth. Defects in ribosome assembly during development cause decreased cell growth and division and developmental disorders. The biogenesis of ribosomes is a complex and highly regulated activity that involves coordinated synthesis, processing, and folding of the ribosomal RNAs (rRNA), and translation, folding, transport, and assembly of the ribosomal proteins. Understanding how the many different steps in ribosome biogenesis are coordinated is a major question in biology.

Ribosomopathies: mechanisms of disease
Ribosomopathies are a collection of childhood disorders in which genetic abnormalities cause impaired ribosome biogenesis and function, resulting in specific clinical phenotypes. These diseases pose a real medical challenge, as the underlying molecular basis for these disorders is almost completely unknown, thereby limiting the treatment of these devastating childhood disorders. We seek to better understand the molecular basis of these diseases.
Tissue-specific roles of ribosomal proteins
Some cells/tissues, but not others, are sensitive to defects in ribosome biogenesis, indicating that ribosomal proteins have unique functions in different tissues or that ribosomes are not made same way; that is, that the process of ribosome biogenesis might be tissue-specific. We are investigating developmental roles and functions of ribosomes during embryogenesis.
Does ribosomal dysfunction drive cancer?
Defects in ribosome biogenesis and/or function are associated with increased cancer predisposition. Cancer cells however are thought to need more translation not less. This apparent paradox challenges our current notion of the role of ribosome biogenesis in cancer and suggests that ribosomal dysfunction/imbalance might unexpectedly facilitate tumor formation. We seek to understand whether defects in ribosome biogenesis can cause an increase in cancer; if so, then explore mechanism of tumorigenesis.
Nucleolus: architecture and assembly
The nucleolus is a membraneless organelle that produces ribosomes. The architecture and assembly of the nucleolus into compartments and sub-compartments relies on the unique physical and chemical properties of nucleolar proteins and nucleic acids. By concentrating molecules into compartments, while allowing dynamic interactions, the nucleolus very efficiently facilitates the complex biochemical reactions that lead to the assembly of ribosomes. We want to understand how organization of cellular matter into this membraneless organelle contributes to the various aspects of its function, particularly to health, aging and disease.
